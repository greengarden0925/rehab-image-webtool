Abstract

Background: AI-generated instructional images offer scalable potential for patient education in rehabilitation, but may contain anatomical errors, unsafe postures, or misleading clinical information. Whether a multi-AI review pipeline—wherein multiple large language models (LLMs) iteratively critique and refine an initial image—reliably improves clinical quality over single-pass generation remains unknown.

Methods: In this double-blind, cross-sectional comparative study, 41 clinicians and health science trainees from three hospital/university teams independently rated 102 paired rehabilitation exercise image sets. A/B assignment was randomised to left/right screen position and counterbalanced across reviewers via a sealed key file. Each pair comprised a directly generated AI image (Image A) and a multi-AI-reviewed version (Image B), covering exercises for nine body regions. A panel of heterogeneous LLMs independently critiqued Image A using a structured schema; aggregated critiques drove a second generation pass to produce Image B. Rating used a 14-item, 5-point Likert instrument spanning clinical accuracy, instructional clarity, patient safety, and clinical adoption intent (q1–q14). Pairwise differences were assessed by Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Bonferroni correction across 14 items; inter-rater reliability by ICC(2,k) (Two-way Random, Absolute Agreement, Average Measures); profession-by-image interactions by linear mixed-effects models with crossed random intercepts for reviewer and exercise (REML, Satterthwaite degrees of freedom); and qualitative defect rates by McNemar's test with separate Bonferroni correction across six defect categories (corrected α=0.0083). The a priori four-construct instrument structure was examined by exploratory confirmatory factor analysis; internal consistency by Cronbach's α.

Results: All 14 rating dimensions significantly favoured Image B (all p<0.001, Bonferroni-corrected). Mean B−A differences ranged from 0.47 (instructional brevity, q5) to 0.91 (visual clarity and safety-related items, q6–q9), with rank-biserial correlations of 0.48–0.73; the largest construct-level effect was for patient safety (rRBC up to 0.73). Overall B preference was expressed by 65.3% of reviewers vs. 12.4% for A (χ²=387.4, p<0.001). For safety-risk defects, B images showed the largest absolute reduction (A 3.6% vs. B 1.7%; McNemar p=0.025, uncorrected), but this did not survive Bonferroni correction across six defect categories (corrected threshold α=0.0083); the trend is consistent with multi-AI review specifically targeting safety-hazard identification. Cronbach's α exceeded 0.94 for all four a priori constructs. ICC(2,k) for Image A total scores was 0.814 (95% CI 0.755–0.864, Good) vs. 0.668 (95% CI 0.565–0.756, Moderate) for Image B; CIs overlap substantially and the lower reliability for B is plausibly attributable to ceiling compression as scores cluster at the upper end of the scale. No reviewer background variable significantly predicted score differences or preference.

Conclusions: A multi-AI review pipeline consistently and substantially improves the clinical quality of AI-generated rehabilitation exercise images across all measured dimensions, with the largest gains in visual clarity, patient safety, and anatomical accuracy. The magnitude of effect is clinically meaningful and robust across professional groups. A safety-risk defect reduction trend was observed but did not survive multiple-comparison correction and requires confirmation in a larger sample. These findings support multi-model review as a candidate minimum quality standard before deploying AI-generated clinical education materials, pending replication and patient-outcome studies.

Keywords: generative artificial intelligence; rehabilitation exercise; patient education; image quality; inter-rater reliability; multi-AI review; large language model; physical therapy

Introduction

Rehabilitation exercise programmes depend critically on clear, anatomically accurate instructional images to guide patient self-practice. Poorly constructed images—depicting incorrect posture, unsafe movement patterns, or misleading anatomical references—have long been identified as a barrier to effective patient education and a potential source of adverse events (Schade et al., 2017; Dhawan et al., 2025). The advent of image-generating artificial intelligence (AI) systems offers an unprecedented opportunity to produce customised instructional visuals at scale, yet the clinical quality of such outputs has not been rigorously validated.

Generative AI, particularly large language model (LLM)-based systems, has demonstrated considerable promise across diverse medical education and clinical support tasks, including ophthalmological board question answering (Cai et al., 2023), radiology report summarisation (Park et al., 2024), and ENT diagnosis support (Hack et al., 2025). However, a consistent limitation across these applications is the occurrence of hallucinations—plausible but factually incorrect outputs—that carry particular risk in clinical image generation, where an erroneous depiction of joint position or muscle activation could promote injury.

A rapidly expanding literature on multi-agent LLM critique offers a conceptual foundation for mitigating such errors. Self-refine approaches—in which an LLM iteratively critiques and revises its own output—have demonstrated measurable improvements in reasoning and generation quality across a range of generative tasks (Madaan et al., 2023). Multi-agent debate frameworks, wherein two or more LLMs adversarially examine candidate outputs, further improve factual accuracy by surfacing disagreements that a single model would overlook (Du et al., 2023). Constitutional AI provides a complementary paradigm in which model-generated critiques anchored to explicit principles guide revision and reduce inaccurate or harmful outputs (Bai et al., 2022). More recently, LLM-as-judge protocols have established that aggregated judgements from multiple LLM evaluators can approximate expert human evaluation across generative tasks (Zheng et al., 2023). However, all of these frameworks have been developed and evaluated primarily in text-only domains; whether the benefits extend to clinical image generation—where anatomical correctness and patient safety impose qualitatively different constraints—remains untested.

One instantiation of these multi-agent principles in the image domain is a multi-AI review pipeline, in which a primary-generation model's output is passed through one or more additional LLMs or vision-language models that identify and correct clinical inaccuracies before finalising the image. This approach parallels human peer-review processes and the ensemble methods that have improved diagnostic AI performance in radiology (Ozyoruk et al., 2025). Recent evidence highlights important limitations of current multimodal LLMs in medical image interpretation, including hallucination rates and suboptimal reasoning for differential diagnoses (Suh et al., 2026), underscoring the need for structured multi-model critique to reduce such errors before clinical deployment. Whether iterative multi-AI review produces measurable improvements in the clinical quality of patient education images, as judged by practising clinicians, has not been empirically tested.

In the present study, we conducted a blinded, multi-rater comparative evaluation of 102 pairs of rehabilitation exercise instructional images, where Image A was generated directly by a single AI pass and Image B underwent an additional multi-AI review and revision cycle. A structured 14-item Likert instrument assessed clinical accuracy, instructional clarity, patient safety, and clinical adoption intent. We hypothesised that Image B would demonstrate significantly superior ratings across all dimensions (H1–H3), that the clinical benefit would be broadly consistent across professional groups (H4), that multi-AI review would reduce defect rates, particularly in safety-relevant categories (H5), and that reviewer background characteristics would not uniformly predict quality judgements (H6). For H6 we explicitly acknowledge that null findings should be interpreted in light of limited statistical power given the available reviewer sample (n=41).

Methods

Study design

This was a double-blind, cross-sectional comparative study using a standardised multi-rater evaluation protocol. Each reviewer independently rated both images from each pair without knowledge of which generation method produced which image. Ethics approval was obtained from [institutional review board; details withheld for review]. All participants provided written informed consent.

Image stimuli

A library of 102 rehabilitation exercise image pairs was developed, covering exercises for nine anatomical regions: cervical/scapular, glenohumeral, core/spine, elbow/wrist flexor, wrist extensor/hand/finger, lumbar/core, hip, knee, and ankle/foot. Image A was produced by a single-pass text-to-image or LLM-assisted generation workflow with a standardised clinical prompt. Image B was produced by submitting Image A—along with the original prompt and clinical context—through the multi-AI review pipeline described below, culminating in a second generation pass. All images were rendered at equal resolution and presented as standardised instructional cards without watermarks, text annotations, or identifiers that could reveal model provenance.

Multi-AI review pipeline

The pipeline comprised three functional roles instantiated by separate model endpoints: a generator, a critic panel, and an aggregator-rewriter. (i) The generator was a state-of-the-art text-to-image model conditioned on a structured clinical prompt template containing the exercise name, target anatomical region, posture description, safety cautions, and visual style constraints. (ii) The critic panel comprised heterogeneous LLM/vision-language models drawn from at least two different model families to maximise critique diversity; each critic independently received the candidate image, the original prompt, and a structured critique schema with seven mandatory fields: anatomical correctness, posture safety, instructional clarity, equipment depiction, demographic appropriateness, environmental plausibility, and overall clinical adoptability. Critic outputs were constrained to a structured JSON schema to enable downstream aggregation. (iii) The aggregator-rewriter consolidated critic outputs using a deterministic union-with-deduplication rule; the union of all distinct critiques was converted into an explicit revision prompt that was concatenated with the original generation prompt and resubmitted to the generator. The cycle was iterated up to a maximum of three rounds; the stopping criterion was critique convergence, operationalised as fewer than two new distinct critique fields contributed by any critic relative to the prior round. Image B for each pair was the final image produced at pipeline termination. The specific model identifiers, version strings, and prompt templates are currently subject to a commercial sensitivity review at our institution; we commit to full public release on acceptance.

Blinding

Blinding was implemented at three levels. First, the A/B label was stripped from all stimuli and held in a sealed key file accessible only to the analysis lead after rating completion. Second, the left/right screen position of A and B within each pair was independently randomised for every reviewer and every pair, and counterbalanced at the panel level so that each generation method appeared equally often on each side across the study. Third, two embedded attention-check pairs (identical images on both sides) were included per reviewer; a priori, reviewers failing both checks were to be excluded, though in practice no reviewer met this criterion. We acknowledge that despite blinding, residual rendering-style cues (line weight, colour palette, compositional regularity) characteristic of iterative multi-pass generation could in principle remain detectable; this is addressed in the Limitations.

Reviewers

A total of 41 reviewers were recruited from three teams: JY (a regional hospital rehabilitation department), TSGH (a medical centre), and YT (a university health sciences programme). Of the 41 reviewers invited, all 41 enrolled, completed at least one rating pair, and were retained in the analysis. Professional composition was: physicians (n=11), physical therapists (n=11), occupational therapists (n=4), health science students (n=14), and other (n=1). The YT team was composed entirely of students, partially confounding team with profession; a sensitivity model including team as a fixed-effect covariate produced inferentially identical conclusions. A reviewer background questionnaire (Google Forms) captured demographics, clinical experience, AI literacy (three Likert items), and AI attitude (five Likert items). Anonymised reviewer IDs were used throughout analysis.

Rating instrument

The 14-item instrument (q1–q14) used a five-point Likert scale (1=strongly disagree to 5=strongly agree) with items grouped into four a priori constructs: (1) clinical accuracy (q1–q4), (2) instructional utility (q5–q8), (3) patient safety (q9–q11), and (4) clinical adoption intent (q12–q14). Two open-text fields captured image-specific defects, subsequently coded into six defect categories: anatomical error, posture/movement error, equipment/environmental error, safety risk, instructional ambiguity, and other. A final preference item (q_compare) asked reviewers to indicate which image they preferred overall (Image A / Image B / about the same).

Statistical analyses

All analyses were performed in Python 3 (pandas, numpy, scipy, statsmodels, semopy, pingouin). For each of the 14 Likert items, the Wilcoxon signed-rank test was applied to the within-pair differences (B−A), with family-wise error controlled by Bonferroni correction across 14 tests (corrected α=0.05/14). Effect sizes were quantified as Cohen's d (on standardised paired differences) and rank-biserial correlation (rRBC). Inter-rater reliability was estimated using ICC(2,k): Two-way Random-effects, Absolute Agreement, Average Measures (Koo & Li, 2016; Shrout & Fleiss, 1979), computed via two-way ANOVA (Type II sums of squares; statsmodels) to accommodate the incomplete block design. The harmonic mean number of raters per exercise was used as k̄ with Spearman-Brown scaling; 95% CIs were derived by the F-distribution method. For preference data, Pearson's χ² and a one-sided binomial test were used; because each reviewer contributed multiple judgements, the χ² is reported as descriptive of the marginal distribution. Defect rates for Images A and B were compared by McNemar's test with Bonferroni correction across the six defect categories (corrected α=0.05/6=0.0083). Linear mixed-effects models (statsmodels MixedLM) were fitted with crossed random intercepts for both reviewer and exercise, using REML estimation. Five-point Likert responses were treated as approximately continuous given the large effective sample size; ordinal cumulative-link mixed models were fitted as a sensitivity analysis and yielded directionally identical inferences. Satterthwaite degrees of freedom were used for p-values; all models achieved convergence without singularity. The a priori four-construct factor structure was examined using exploratory confirmatory factor analysis (semopy); Cronbach's α was reported within each construct. Associations between reviewer background and B−A score difference or B-preference rate were assessed by Mann-Whitney U, Kruskal-Wallis H, and Spearman's ρ.

Missing data

The four partially incomplete exercises were retained in all analyses using available-case estimation; pairwise deletion was applied for Wilcoxon and McNemar tests. For mixed-effects models, all available reviewer-exercise records contributed to estimation via REML under missing-at-random assumptions. A sensitivity analysis restricted to the 98 fully complete exercises produced materially identical results.

The effect on patient safety items was consistently among the largest observed.

H4: Mixed-effects analysis of profession-by-image interaction

Linear mixed-effects models treating exercise identity as a random effect confirmed a significant main effect of image type (B vs. A) for all 14 items (all z>4.95, p<0.001). Profession-by-image interaction terms were largely non-significant. The only notable exception was q3 (anatomical detail accuracy) for occupational therapists (β=−0.53, z=−2.69, p=0.007), indicating that this group showed a smaller B−A advantage on anatomical accuracy relative to the reference group (students). All other profession-image interactions were p>0.10. These findings support the hypothesis that the quality advantage of Image B is broadly consistent across professional groups.

Overall preference (q_compare)

Of 814 responses with a valid preference: Image B was preferred by 532 (65.3%), Image A by 101 (12.4%), and 181 (22.2%) rated both images as approximately equal (Table 3). Pearson's χ² was 387.4 (df=2, p<0.001). A one-sided binomial test for B exceeding A was also highly significant (p<0.001).

Stratified by body region, B-preference ranged from 51.2% (core/spine) to 72.9% (cervical/scapular) (Figure 4). B-preference exceeded 60% for eight of nine regions.

H5: Defect category analysis

Reviewer-annotated defect categories included: (1) anatomical error, (2) movement execution error, (3) equipment error, (4) safety risk, (5) violation of clinical guidelines, and (6) textual labelling error. Among the six coded categories, safety risk showed the largest absolute reduction in B images (A rate 3.6% vs. B rate 1.7%; McNemar p=0.025, uncorrected; Table 4). After Bonferroni correction across the six defect categories (corrected threshold α=0.05/6=0.0083), no category reached the corrected significance level. The safety-risk reduction is therefore reported as a directional trend consistent with the hypothesis that multi-AI review specifically targets safety-hazard identification, rather than as a corrected significant finding. No other defect category showed a significant uncorrected difference; some categories showed slightly higher numerical rates in B, plausibly reflecting stricter scrutiny applied by reviewers to the more polished B images (Zacharias et al., 2026).

Inter-rater reliability

ICC(2,k) for the 14-item total score was 0.814 (95% CI 0.755–0.864; Good) for Image A and 0.668 (95% CI 0.565–0.756; Moderate) for Image B, based on an average of k̄=8.3 reviewers per exercise (Table 5). Item-level ICCs for Image A rated 10 of 14 dimensions as Good (≥0.75) and four as Moderate; for Image B, all 14 dimensions were Moderate (0.58–0.67). The 95% CIs for total-score ICC overlap substantially (A upper bound 0.864, B upper bound 0.756; B lower bound 0.565), and the apparent difference is therefore presented descriptively rather than as an inferentially established distinction. A plausible explanation for the lower ICC in B is ceiling compression: as Image B scores cluster at the upper end of the scale, between-exercise variance shrinks relative to residual reviewer variance, mechanically reducing ICC even when agreement is maintained. Formal comparison of the dependent ICCs would require bootstrap resampling and is recommended for future work.

H6: Reviewer background characteristics and rating behaviour

No reviewer background variable significantly predicted mean B−A score difference or B-preference rate (all Mann-Whitney U, Kruskal-Wallis, and Spearman ρ tests: p>0.05 after adjustment). Variables examined included sex, age group, educational level, years of clinical experience, AI use frequency, number of patients per week, frequency of using instructional materials, AI literacy (three-item scale; PCA composite), and AI attitude (five-item scale; PCA composite). The highest observed magnitude was for AI use frequency vs. B-preference rate (Spearman ρ=−0.291, p=0.065), which did not reach statistical significance. Given the limited reviewer sample (n=41), these null findings should be interpreted as inconclusive rather than as evidence of no effect; we cannot exclude moderate associations detectable only in larger samples.

Instrument internal consistency

Cronbach's α for Image B ratings was 0.961 (accuracy construct, q1–q4), 0.966 (utility, q5–q8), 0.979 (safety, q9–q11), and 0.949 (adoption, q12–q14), indicating excellent internal consistency for all four latent constructs. These findings validate the theoretical structure of the rating instrument.

Discussion

Principal findings

This study provides the first blinded, multi-rater empirical evidence that a multi-AI review pipeline systematically and substantially improves the clinical quality of AI-generated rehabilitation exercise instructional images. The magnitude of improvement was clinically meaningful across all four assessed constructs—clinical accuracy, instructional utility, patient safety, and clinical adoption intent—and was consistent across nine anatomical regions and all professional backgrounds evaluated.

The effect was largest for items related to visual clarity and patient safety (B−A≈0.91 points, rRBC≈0.70–0.73), and smallest for items concerning instructional brevity and factual specificity (B−A≈0.47–0.49 points). This pattern is interpretable: single-pass AI generation frequently produces images with visually inconsistent lighting, disproportionate anatomy, or ambiguous joint positions—defects that are relatively obvious to clinical reviewers and readily identified by a reviewing LLM prompted to check anatomical correctness. In contrast, brevity and specificity of instructional text require domain-specific pedagogical knowledge that a generalist review model may not be optimally equipped to refine.

Defect profiles and safety

Among the six coded defect categories, the safety-risk category showed the largest absolute reduction in B images (A 3.6% vs. B 1.7%; McNemar p=0.025, uncorrected). After Bonferroni correction across six categories (corrected threshold α=0.0083), this difference does not reach the corrected significance level and should be interpreted as a directional trend rather than a confirmed finding. Nevertheless, the pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that structured multi-AI critique, in which each critic model is prompted to flag safety-relevant failure modes, preferentially reduces the most consequential defect type. AI-generated images depicting unsafe exercise postures—for example, excessive lumbar hyperextension during a hamstring stretch or unprotected weight-bearing in a post-surgical patient—carry the potential for patient harm if deployed without verification (Guo et al., 2025; Wung et al., 2026). Confirmation in a larger powered sample with direct patient-safety annotations is warranted.

The lack of significant differences in anatomical error rates (category 1) and movement execution errors (category 2) between Images A and B is notable. One interpretation is that the multi-AI review process, as implemented, may be less effective at detecting subtle three-dimensional anatomical inaccuracies or complex multi-joint movement patterns, a hypothesis that requires targeted future evaluation with annotation by anatomically expert raters. This is consistent with recent evidence that multimodal LLMs substantially underperform specialist clinicians in medical image interpretation, with hallucination in 4.5% of diagnostic cases and only 29.4% of LLM analyses rated as acceptable (Suh et al., 2026).

Inter-rater reliability and ceiling compression

The finding that ICC was descriptively higher for Image A (Good, 0.814; 95% CI 0.755–0.864) than Image B (Moderate, 0.668; 95% CI 0.565–0.756) may initially appear counterintuitive. The 95% CIs overlap substantially, and we therefore present this as a descriptive observation rather than an inferentially established difference; a formal comparison of dependent ICCs would require bootstrap resampling. A plausible, though not directly tested, explanation is ceiling compression: when images are rated predominantly in the upper portion of the scale (Image B mean ratings 3.77–4.02 vs. 2.89–3.52 for Image A), between-exercise variance shrinks relative to residual reviewer variance, mechanically reducing ICC. This phenomenon has been documented in reliability research on high-quality stimuli (Novak et al., 2021). The moderate reliability of Image B ratings may therefore reflect a statistical consequence of compressed variance at higher quality levels rather than unreliable reviewer judgements.

From a practical standpoint, moderate ICC for Image B is acceptable for its intended purpose—screening for clinical suitability—when averaged across eight or more reviewers, which is the study design used here. Single-reviewer use would reduce to the ICC(2,1) value (range 0.145–0.199 for Image B), which is insufficient for high-stakes clinical decisions without consensus review.

Universality of the quality advantage across professions

The near-absence of significant profession-by-image interaction effects (all p>0.10 except q3 for occupational therapists) indicates that the quality advantage of multi-AI review is perceived consistently by physicians, physical therapists, occupational therapists, and students alike. This cross-professional consistency strengthens the generalisability of the finding and suggests that multi-AI review addresses deficiencies that are recognisable at a basic clinical visual literacy level rather than requiring subspecialty knowledge to detect (Guo et al., 2025).

The single nominal interaction (q3 × occupational therapy; p=0.007) must be interpreted with caution given the small OT subgroup (n=4); this interaction is fragile and may not replicate. It may reflect domain-specific perceptions of anatomical detail relevant to upper-extremity function—a core competency of occupational therapy practice—where Image B's improvements were less salient relative to discipline-specific standards. Investigation in larger samples with sufficient power for subgroup analysis is warranted.

Reviewer background and AI literacy

The absence of any significant association between reviewer background variables and rating behaviour is reassuring from an implementation standpoint: it suggests that clinicians without AI expertise are as capable as AI-experienced clinicians of identifying the quality differential between single-pass and multi-AI-reviewed images. This supports the use of standard clinical review panels—rather than AI-specialised teams—as the appropriate evaluators of AI-generated clinical education materials, consistent with the finding that healthcare professionals can meaningfully assess AI tool acceptability in oncology nursing contexts (Zacharias et al., 2026).

Implications for clinical implementation

Our findings have several direct implications:

  1. Multi-AI review is a candidate minimum quality assurance step before AI-generated rehabilitation images are distributed to patients, pending replication and patient-outcome evidence. The consistent B−A advantage—even at its smallest (q5, 0.47 points)—represents a clinically perceptible difference on a 5-point scale and justifies the additional computational cost of the review pipeline.
  2. Safety-risk screening should be explicitly included in review prompts. The observed safety-risk reduction trend (McNemar p=0.025, uncorrected) is consistent with this recommendation even though it did not survive multiple-comparison correction. Structured safety-hazard checklists within critic prompts may help make this effect more robust.
  3. Human review remains necessary given the moderate ICC for Image B under single-reviewer conditions and the residual anatomical error rates. The multi-AI pipeline improves image quality but does not substitute for clinician-led final approval, consistent with recommendations for hybrid AI–human oversight in medical applications (Heinke et al., 2024).
  4. Region-specific variation in the B−A advantage (lowest for core/spine; highest for cervical/scapular and glenohumeral) suggests that anatomical complexity moderates review effectiveness, and that regional calibration of review prompts may further improve outcomes.

Practical deployment considerations

Adopting multi-AI review imposes additional computational cost (multiple model passes per image), latency (iterative critique-revise cycles extending per-image generation time several-fold), and operational governance burden (model selection, version-update cadence, prompt-template change control). Health systems considering deployment will need to weigh these costs against the observed quality gains and establish institutional policies for periodic re-validation as upstream models evolve. The environmental cost of inference at scale across large image libraries should also be considered in deployment planning.

Limitations

Several limitations qualify our findings. First, the YT team comprised entirely students, creating a partial confound between team and profession; a team-covariate sensitivity analysis yielded identical conclusions. Second, pipeline test-retest stability was not formally assessed; stochastic generation may produce non-identical Image B outputs across runs, and the magnitude of this variability is unknown. Third, the specific AI models used for generation and review are withheld pending commercial sensitivity review, with full release committed at acceptance; replication studies should document model identifiers and prompt templates. Fourth, we did not measure patient-level outcomes (comprehension, adherence, safety events), and recommendations about deployment standards therefore rest on intermediate clinician-judged quality endpoints. Fifth, the underlying models are subject to version drift; conclusions may not hold for substantially updated model versions, and periodic re-validation on fixed snapshots is recommended. Sixth, although the rating panel did not include pipeline developers, developers and raters were institutionally adjacent, and indirect cultural influences on rating norms cannot be fully excluded. Seventh, the rehabilitation context and rating instrument are specific to a Traditional Chinese-speaking Taiwan healthcare setting, constraining generalisability to other regions and languages. Additional caveats include: defect coding was derived from open text and is subject to coder interpretation; the study is limited to rehabilitation exercise images and may not generalise to other clinical visual modalities; residual rendering-style cues could in principle have provided unintended A/B signal despite blinding; and the q3 × OT interaction must be interpreted with caution given the small OT subgroup (n=4).

Conclusions

Multi-AI review of AI-generated rehabilitation exercise images produces clinically meaningful and statistically robust improvements in clinical accuracy, instructional utility, patient safety, and clinical adoption intent, as judged by a diverse panel of 41 clinicians and trainees across three institutions. The quality advantage is consistent across all nine body regions and all professional groups evaluated. A safety-risk defect reduction was observed as a directional trend but did not survive Bonferroni correction across six defect categories, and requires confirmation in a larger powered sample. Pending replication and patient-outcome evidence, these findings support multi-AI review pipelines as a candidate minimum quality assurance step before deployment of AI-generated patient education materials in clinical rehabilitation settings, accompanied by transparent model-version governance and human clinical oversight.
